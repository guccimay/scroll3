2 E. Redwood Street

Building History
1. North German Lloyd Steamship Line
2. Savings Bank Building
3. BMA Downtown Gallery
4. (Returned) Savings Bank
5. FedEx Kinko's



Now operating as a FedEx Kinko’s, 2 E. Redwood Street,
nicknamed the “Hansa Haus,” retains its original German
Renaissance architecture amidst the office buildings
surrounding it. Harkening to the influx of immigrants
from Germany to Locust Point in the early 20th century,
the Hansa Haus was built in 1912 as the Baltimore office
of the North German Lloyd Steamship Company, resembling
“a half-timbered 16th-century German Rathaus, perhaps
the Zwicken in Halberstadt.” Three stories high, the
building was constructed in the North German architectural
style and includes 39 coats of arms of Hanseatic on its
stucco walls, and it remains surprisingly unchanged today.

In 1973, the Baltimore Museum of Art opened the doors
of the Hansa Haus to reveal the new Downtown Gallery.
Owned by the Savings Bank of Baltimore at this point,
the building provided a rent-free space for the museum
to expand programming and enlarge audience. Downtown
Gallery represented the country’s first satellite gallery
andquickly attracted workers from the surrounding office
buildings with a variety of programs surrounding food,
dance, film screenings, and lectures to accompany the
exhibitions in the space. Roughly 36,000 people visited
the Downtown Gallery in its first year. The Downtown Gallery
has somehow since escaped the city’s memory, despite its
success, but remains unique in its accessibility and availability.

Brenda Edelson acted as the Director of Downtown Gallery
from 1971 until the gallery’s closure in 1975 when the
Savings Bank of Baltimore decided to move back into the
space. During that time, more than thirty exhibitions and
events occurred in the space.

The Contemporary: What were some of the most memorable
shows you organized?

Brenda Edelson: Toulouse Lautrec show and a photojournalism show that was circulated by the Museum of Modern Art called Behind the Picture Press. For the Toulouse Lautrec exhibition, we had Linda Brown making crepes and can-can dancers in the street, and it was great fun. I usually either had a speaker or films that related to the exhibition.

TC: Were there any other exciting projects going on
nearby at the time?

BE: The Downtown Gallery was wonderful, but it takes
a lot of energy and money, and nobody has it today to
do that sort of thing. George Dalsheimer ran a beautiful
gallery downtown on Charles Street and they were open
quite a while, and then I had a gallery briefly on
Charles Street, after I left the museum with a partner,
and we showed contemporary prints.. The Dalsheimer Gallery
had world class photography exhibits, and it went on for
probably ten years. I would think it closed around 1985.
We showed contemporary fine prints, so we had shows of work from Frank Stella. I just did not enjoy the gallery business.

We had a Frankenthaler show, a Hockney show, a Motherwell show. It was during the print boom, when prints were getting bigger and more expensive, and we had a very good success, I just did not like retail, doing it that way. I became a private dealer afterwards. The Baltimore Museum also had a wonderful Sales and Rentals Gallery, and it was for many years a very successful program that got a lot of people in Baltimore collecting. They could rent a print for three months at a time and then buy it or return it. It gave them chance to see how they liked living with something. Wonderful print shows, photography shows, drawing shows. It was a great effort. But it was before things got so very expensive. It was during the years when things were more affordable and approachable.

Another 1974 exhibition entitled, “Highlights from the Atlanta University Collection of Afro-American Art,” presented work from the High Museum’s collection, which boasted the largest number of works by contemporary black artists at the time. Hale Woodruff, Vivian Williams, Henry O. Tanner, Elizabeth Catlett, and Jacob Lawrence are among over sixty artists presented. Supported by the Baltimore Afro, the exhibition was supplemented with gallery talks, lectures, and two film series, “Of Black America” and “Black Artists in America.”
